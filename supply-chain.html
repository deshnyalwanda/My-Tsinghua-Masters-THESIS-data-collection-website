Nuclear Power Plant Supply Chain: Materials and Services

This section outlines the key materials and services needed for nuclear power plant construction and operation. It identifies potential areas where Kenyan industries can supply these materials and services, or build capacity to do so. The information is based on a global outlook of completed nuclear power projects.

Please note: The information provided serves only as a guide to help industries understand some of the services and materials required in the construction of a nuclear power plant. A nuclear power plant has numerous components. Even though the project is highly specialized, requiring many components to be imported, some components can be manufactured within the nation. The nuclear power plant is divided into three main sections:

  • Nuclear island
  • Turbine island
  • Balance of Plant (BOP)

1. Nuclear Island: The Heart of the Reactor (Highly Specialized – Requires International Partnerships)

The nuclear island contains the reactor vessel and primary cooling system. It needs extreme precision and quality control. International partnerships are crucial for the entire section with very minimal or no local participation.

1.1. Reactor Pressure Vessels (RPVs)

Installation of Fuqing Unit 6 HPR1000 Reactor (1000MWe) in China's Fujian province Source: China National Nuclear Commission

The RPV is a thick-walled steel vessel that holds the nuclear fuel. It is very difficult to make and requires special manufacturing skills.A typical RPV like the HPR1000 has an inside diameter of 4.6 meters and height of 12 meters, weighing 330 tons. Making it requires huge forging presses of over 10,000 tonnes capacity.

According to a 2018 Department of Energy report, even though the United States leads in global nuclear power geneartion, it relies on foreign suppliers for large nuclear components for its new reactors due to high costs.

For instance, the projected cost for establishing a domestic ultra-large forging facility capable of producing these components in the U.S. was approximately $2 billion (KSh 255.14 billion). A single forged pressure vessel can cost around $150 million (KSh 19.14 billion) to produce, with only a few companies worldwide capable of such fabrication.

Key international manufacturers include:

  • Doosan Enerbility (South Korea)
  • Japan Steel Works (Japan)
  • China First Heavy Industries (China)

Local Potential: Kenyan companies can provide support structures, handling equipment, and installation help under strict supervision.

1.2. Steam Generators

Steam Generator manufactured by Framatome delivered to Cruas-Meyss, France Source: World Nuclear News

Steam generators transfer heat from the reactor to make steam for electricity. They are huge heat exchangers.Typical steam generators are 21 meters tall and weigh up to 800 tons. Each contains 3,000 to 16,000 tubes. Major manufacturers include Shanghai Electric and Mitsubishi Heavy Industries.

Local Potential: Support structures, handling equipment, and installation under supervision.

1.3. Primary Coolant Pumps

These pumps circulate high-pressure water through the reactor core. They must be extremely reliable. The pumps are 5–8 meters tall, weigh 50–100 tons, and use large motors up to 6.3 MW. They can deliver flow rates of over 20,000 m3/h at pressures up to 15 MPa. Only a few companies worldwide make nuclear-grade coolant pumps that meet strict safety standards.

A Primary coolant pump. Source: World Nuclear News

Local Potential: Support structures, handling equipment, and installation under supervision.

1.4. Containment Buildings

Image showing complete concreting of containment building at Zhangzhou 1 NPP in China's Fujian province Source: World Nuclear News

The containment building houses the reactor and safety systems. It is designed to withstand earthquakes and other extreme events including 400 ton aircraft crash.

Modern containments, such as the AP1000’s 45-meter-diameter, 65-meter-high steel-concrete hybrid, feature 1.2–2.4-meter-thick reinforced concrete walls with a 6–40 mm carbon steel liner (ASTM A516). The structure uses approximately 40,000 m3 of high-density concrete (typically 70 MPa) and over 5,000 metric tons of high-strength rebar (A615 Grade 75). The Chinese 3rd generation 1000MW reactor Hualong 1 uses containment vessel of diameter 46m and height 69m. It has two robust concrete shells of 1.3 m and 1.8m. This results in an enclosed space of 80,000 m3. For every reactor, there is a containment building.

For Kenya's nuclear plant, cement and steel companies must meet special requirements:

Special radiation-shielding concrete is needed:

  • High-density concrete with special aggregates like barite or magnetite
  • Compressive strength of 150–180 MPa
  • Low porosity for radiation protection

Typical steel requirements include

  • High-strength rebar (ASTM A615 Grade 60 or 75)
  • Steel plates (ASTM A36 or A516) for lining

Local Potential: Kenya has good potential in concrete batching, rebar fabrication, and formwork. Special techniques will need international partnerships.

1.5. Other Critical Components

Fuel assemblies, Fuel rods, Control Rods, Valves and Piping

Fuel assemblies contain multiple fuel rods. The fuel rods contain uranium oxide fuel pellets. They are made of zirconium alloys and require precision manufacturing. Control rods absorb neutrons to control the nuclear reaction. They use materials like boron carbide or hafnium.

Image showing a fuel assembly. Contains fuel rods containing uranium oxide fuel pellets. Source: World Nuclear Association

Local Potential: Limited - highly specialized manufacturing.

Nuclear-grade valves and piping must withstand high pressure, temperature, and radiation.

Local Potential: Basic support piping and installation under supervision.

1.6. Welding Requirements for Nuclear Island

Welding in nuclear plants must meet the highest quality standards. All welds are carefully inspected using non-destructive testing methods.

Required Certifications and Standards:

Nuclear welding requires special certifications beyond standard welding qualifications:

  • ASME Section IX (American Society of Mechanical Engineers) - Qualification standard for welding and brazing procedures
  • RCC-M Code (French nuclear design and construction rules) - Design and construction rules for mechanical components of PWR nuclear islands
  • AWS Certified Welding Inspector (CWI) - American Welding Society certification for welding inspectors
  • Rigorous non-destructive testing including:
    • Radiographic (X-ray) Testing - ASTM E1742 Standard Practice for Radiographic Examination
    • ASNT SNT-TC-1A - Standard for qualification and certification of NDT personnel

    The construction of Fangjiashan NPP (2×1000MW, 2008-2015) provides valuable insights into welding requirements for large-scale nuclear projects:

    Key Welding Statistics from Fangjiashan NPP:

    The welding operation for the Primary System was at a massive scale, typical for nuclear power plant projects. This included over 15,000 critical welds, which necessitated specialized procedures and qualifications for the 120 major structural welds on the Reactor Pressure Vessel. Furthermore, the 3,200 meters of primary piping welds were subjected to stringent 100% radiographically tested quality assurance. The the 8,500 square meters of steel liner welding demanded strict leak-tight integrity testing.

    • Containment Liner: Large-scale structural welding with 100% volumetric examination

    1.7. Material Handling Equipment for Nuclear Island

    Special equipment is needed to handle large, heavy nuclear components safely.

    Cranes

    • Crawler Cranes: For heaviest components (RPV, Steam generator) (1,000+ tons capacity).
    • Tower Cranes: For construction at high heights (up to 50 tons capacity).
    • Gantry Cranes: For moving heavy objects in specific areas i.e turbine generator (up to 500 tons).
    • Ring and Gantry Cranes: Special cranes within the containment building (1,000+ tons capacity).

    Other Handling Equipment

    • Heavy Haul Trucks: Multi-axle trailers for transport (400+ tons)
    • Self-Propelled Modular Transporters (SPMTs): For precise movement of huge components.
    • Lifting Gear: Special slings, shackles, and spreader beams.
    • Clean Room Cranes: For handling sensitive equipment. Smaller overhead cranes (5-20 ton capacity) with precision controls for delicate component handling.

    The Fangjiashan Nuclear Power Plant (2×1000MW, constructed 2008-2015) provides a relevant case study for equipment requirements.

    1.8. Human Resource Requirements

    Operating a nuclear power plant requires a rigorously trained, highly disciplined workforce. Globally, nuclear operators must have a background in STEM, typically mechanical, electrical, chemical, or nuclear engineering. This is before receiving specialized nuclear training. This model is followed in China, Pakistan, the UAE, and all mature nuclear operating countries.

    Operator Training Requirements (International Standards)

    Operators must meet the following prerequisites before entering formal reactor training programs:

    • A bachelor's degree in engineering (mechanical, electrical, chemical, nuclear) or another STEM field (IAEA Safety Reports Series No. 50).
    • Basic training in thermodynamics, reactor physics, and radiation protection (IAEA TECDOC-1057).

    After meeting academic requirements, operators undergo structured training programs. According to the IAEA and nuclear vendor documentation, a typical reactor operator training pathway includes:

    • Classroom Training (6–12 months): Reactor systems, nuclear safety, thermohydraulics, and procedures (IAEA NP-T-3.12).
    • Simulator Training (6–12 months): Full-scope control room simulations required by IAEA Safety Standards (IAEA SSG-39).
    • On-the-Job Training: Hands-on work under licensed senior operators (IAEA Nuclear Energy Series NG-T-2.8).
    • Licensing Exams: Mandatory theoretical and practical examinations overseen by the national regulator.

    Pakistan's operators for the K-2 and K-3 Hualong One reactors (built by CNNC) received extensive training at:

    • CNNC Training Centers including the Fuqing NPP full-scope Hualong One simulator (World Nuclear News, 2021).
    • China National Nuclear Corporation (CNNC) Nuclear Power Institute of China (NPIC) for advanced systems training (CNNC Official Website).

    CNNC confirmed publicly that Pakistani engineers received multi-year operational training in China before the units were commissioned.

    All personnel must also complete safety training that includes the following:

    • Radiation protection (IAEA GSR Part 3)
    • Human performance and safety culture (INSAG-4)
    • Emergency preparedness (IAEA GS-R-2)
    • Quality assurance (QA/QC) (IAEA GS-G-3.1)

    This expanded human resource framework reflects validated staffing models from IAEA publications, CNNC training documentation, and global best practices from new nuclear programs such as Pakistan's Hualong One units and the UAE's Barakah plant.

2. Turbine Island: Power Generation (Conventional Tech – Localization Opportunities)

The Turbine Island consisits of the turbine and generator.The island functions to converts high-pressure steam from the Nuclear Island's steam generators into rotational mechanical energy (turbine), which is then converted into electrical energy by the generator. While the design is nuclear-specific, the core manufacturing technology is non-nuclear. This offers significant localization potential; Particularly in the services, as the machinery are still significantly highly specialized to be sourced from local manufacturers.

Industry Potential
Construction & Civil Works High Potential: Extensive civil and structural works for turbine foundations, building, and support structures
Material Handling High Potential: Operation of large gantry cranes and heavy equipment for component installation
Welding & Fabrication Moderate Potential: Fabrication of non-nuclear piping, tanks, support structures, and heat exchanger shells
Technical Support High Potential: Electrical wiring, installation, and commissioning of conventional systems (e.g., generator auxiliaries, switchgear)

2.1. Steam Turbines and Generators

The massive Steam turbine and generator form the "Conventional Island" of the nuclear power plant. This means its a section that is typically found in many other types of power generating plants such as Geothermal and thermal plants.

Image showing inside a Nuclear Power Plant's turbine hall. Source: Bloomberg

The Steam Turbine must operate continuously and reliably for maximum base-load power generation. For large 1000 MWe nuclear power plants, low-speed (1,500 rpm or 1,800 rpm) turbines, such as GE Vernova's Arabelle, are frequently selected for their high reliability, robustness, and resistance to blade erosion, evidenced by their use in major new-build projects like the Hinkley Point C project in the UK and the Akkuyu Nuclear Power Plant in Turkey. The Generator is coupled to the turbine and converts this mechanical energy into electricity, which is then fed to the grid.

  • Both the turbine and generator are designed for the full 60-year operational life of the nuclear plant.
  • Major Maintenance (overhauls) is typically scheduled during refueling outages. Typically on every second to third fuelling cycle, usually every four to six years, requiring the dismantling, inspection, and possible replacement of internal components like turbine blades and generator windings.
  • Routine Maintenance is continuous and condition-based, involving monitoring of lubrication systems, temperature, and vibration analysis.

These highly complex components are manufactured by a few global technology leaders, including:

  • Shanghai Electric: A major domestic supplier supporting the vast expansion of China's nuclear fleet.
  • Mitsubishi Heavy Industries (MHI): Supplied key turbine equipment for the world's first AP1000 reactors at the Sanmen and Haiyang Nuclear Power Stations in China.
  • Doosan Enerbility: Has supplied components to numerous domestic South Korean plants and the Barakah plant in the UAE.
  • GE Vernova
  • Siemens Energy
  • Ansaldo Energia

Local Potential: While the main turbine-generator sets are imported, local industry can support in the construction of deep turbine foundations and the installation support for this heavy machinery. Local electrical firms can handle the installation, testing, and commissioning of the generator's auxiliary and control systems.

2.2. Condensers, Feedwater Heaters, and Moisture Separator Reheaters (MSRs)

These large heat exchangers and steam processors are essential components of the Conventional Island, working to maximize thermodynamic efficiency by recovering heat and drying steam before it returns to the turbine or the reactor system.

Condensers: These are massive shell-and-tube heat exchangers located below the low-pressure turbine. Their primary function is to cool exhausted steam back into water (condensate). The tubes must be highly resistant to corrosion from cooling water (seawater, river water, etc.). Common materials used in their construction include Titanium, Stainless Steel (316L), or Copper-nickel alloys. The outer condenser structures/shells are typically made using carbon steel.

Image showing a Nuclear Power Plant's condenser units outside a turbine hall. Source: Nooter Construction

Feedwater Heaters:These are shell-and-tube heat exchangers that use extracted steam from the turbine stages to pre-heat the condensate. This process significantly improves the overall plant efficiency by reducing the heat required from the reactor/steam generators. They require precise tube-to-tubesheet welding during fabrication.

Moisture Separator Reheaters (MSRs):These are critical for high-reliability turbine operation. Located between the high-pressure and low-pressure turbine sections, MSRs remove moisture from the steam and reheat it, which is essential for protecting low-pressure turbine blades from erosion caused by wet steam. They utilize stainless steel components.

Local Potential in Fabrication and Supply:

Although this equipment is highly specialized, its non-nuclear nature presents a significant opportunity for local participation and supply chain integration.

The materials, such as the tubes which are sometimes made of Titanium, require highly specialized manufacturing processes. This involves specialized machinery, such as high-precision tube drawing and rolling mills and advanced orbital welding systems for seamless fabrication.

Manufacturing these specific items without pre-existing, certified machinery results in 'First of a Kind' (FOAK) equipment, thereby raising initial capital costs and requiring substantial human resource training.

The condenser shells, often made of heavy-duty carbon steel, can potentially be manufactured in Kenya, as there are currently existing local companies with years of experience in fabricating pressure vessels and heat exchangers. However, further research is needed to determine the precise manufacturing capacity of local firms, including the maximum specifications (e.g., diameters, thicknesses, and tolerances) of existing plate rollers and bending machines.

This information is essential for formulating effective policy and increasing cooperation between local industry firms, the government, and the potential NPP supplier. In summary, there are significant opportunities for local industry to participate in the supply chain:

2.3. Cooling Water and Auxiliary Piping Systems

Extensive piping is required for circulating cooling water, condensate, and high-pressure steam lines, typically about 2km in length.

The materials used in piping include Carbon steel (ASTM A106, A53) and Stainless steel (ASTM A312). High-pressure steam lines use creep-resistant low-alloy steels (e.g., P22, P91).

Local Potential: Local manufacturers can supply a substantial portion of the conventional piping components (fittings, flanges) and provide the specialized installation and non-destructive testing (NDE) services.

2.4. Welding Requirements for Turbine Island (Conventional Power Standards)

Welding must adhere to conventional power plant codes, though with strict Quality Assurance/Quality Control (QA/QC) protocols to ensure the integrity of the power generation cycle.

Required Certifications and Standards:

Welding procedures must meet the following standards, which are essential for pressure boundary and structural integrity:

  • High-Pressure Piping: Welding procedures must comply with ASME B31.1 (Power Piping). This involves pre-heat and post-weld heat treatment (PWHT) for creep-resistant alloy steels used in main steam lines.
  • Heat Exchangers: Tube-to-tubesheet welds require automated orbital welding for high integrity.
  • Structural Welding: Welds for supports and general structures must comply with AWS D1.1 (Structural Welding Code).
  • Pressure Boundary Welds: Welders and inspectors must hold ASME Section IX qualifications for pressure boundary welds.

2.5. Material Handling Equipment (Turbine Island Specific)

The primary handling requirement is for the installation and overhaul of the generator and turbine rotors. Local industry has high potential in the operation of this large gantry crane and heavy equipment for component installation.

2.6. Human Resource Requirements (Turbine Island)

Specialized technical staff with experience in large rotating machinery and thermal power generation are critical.

  • Turbine Operators: Licensed personnel specializing in turbine startup, load changes, and shutdown procedures.
  • I&C Technicians: Responsible for the calibration and maintenance of the conventional control systems (I&C) that regulate the turbine and generator auxiliaries. The design emphasizes uniformity for improved maintenance.
  • Mechanical Maintenance Engineers: Experts in precision alignment and balancing of high-speed rotating equipment, including turbine rotors and pump sets.

The Balance of Plant (BOP) encompasses all auxiliary systems and infrastructure outside the Nuclear and Turbine Islands. This section provides the highest percentage of local content opportunity due to its use of conventional, non-nuclear specific technology.

3.1. BOP Systems Overview (Reference: 1000 MWe Class Thermal Plant)

The BOP systems for a nuclear plant mirror the auxiliary systems of a large-scale fossil-fuel thermal power project, such as a 2x(500 MWe or above) facility.

Image showing some of the civil construction works at a Akkuyu NPP in Turkiye. Source: Daily Sabah

These systems are essential for plant functionality but do not handle radioactive materials. The systems include:

  • Circulating Water System (CWS): Large pumps and piping for moving cooling water from the water source (or cooling towers) to the condenser. Requires large-scale civil structures and deep foundations for pump houses.
  • Fire Protection, Detection & Alarm System: Includes pumps, piping, valves, and control panels required for plant safety.
  • Water Treatment Plant (WTP): Systems for treating raw water for use as boiler feed/make-up water (demineralization, reverse osmosis).
  • Fuel Oil Handling System: Storage and transfer system for diesel generators and emergency boilers.

3.2. Cooling Towers and Auxiliary Buildings (Civil Works Opportunities)

Cooling towers are large, complex civil structures designed to efficiently dissipate waste heat from the plant. However, it is not a must for a nuclear power plant to have a cooling tower, especially if it is constructed next to a large water body.

Image showing a Nuclear Power Plant cooling tower. Source: Duke Energy

  • Materials: Construction predominantly uses mass quantities of reinforced concrete (25-40 MPa) and carbon steel rebar (A615). The internal fill material is typically PVC.
  • Local Potential: Local cement manufacturers, aggregate suppliers, concrete batching plants, and civil construction firms have the capability to undertake the full scope of cooling tower and auxiliary building construction. Strict adherence to quality assurance (QA/QC) is still mandatory.

3.3. Transformers and Switchyards (Electrical & Logistics)

This infrastructure provides the critical link between the plant's generator and the national electrical transmission grid, covering the main step-up transformers and the high-voltage switchyard equipment.

Component Materials and Specifications:

The main electrical equipment requires highly specialized materials to handle extreme voltages and ensure plant safety:

  • Transformers: Utilize specialized materials such as silicon steel cores, copper or aluminum windings, and large carbon steel tanks.
  • Switchyard Components: Require high-conductivity copper or aluminum busbars and high-voltage porcelain or composite insulators. Structural supports are constructed from steel alloys.
  • Electrical Wiring/Cabling: All cables must meet specialized standards for fire retardancy, low smoke, halogen-free, and radiation resistance (where applicable), utilizing insulation types such as XLPE, PVC, EPR, mica tape, ceramic silicone rubber, or PTFE.

There are currently no manufacturers in Kenya capable of producing the specialized, high-capacity, high-voltage transformers (e.g., Main Step-Up Transformers) required for a nuclear power plant. These critical components must be imported.

  • Local Potential (Services): Local electrical engineering firms can manage the comprehensive installation, testing, and commissioning of the switchyard components, including cable laying, termination, and system integration.
  • Local Potential (Fabrication): Opportunities exist for local fabrication of auxiliary supporting infrastructure, such as non-critical steel transformer tanks (under strategic technological partnerships), cable trays, and busbar supports.

3.4. Welding Requirements for Balance of Plant (BOP)

Welding in the BOP mainly focuses on conventional construction codes, providing a larger scope for certified local contractors.

  • Structural Welding: All steel structures for buildings, pipe racks, switchyard supports, and water intake structures must comply with AWS D1.1 (Structural Welding Code).
  • Process Piping: Non-safety related process lines (e.g., domestic water, fire protection, low-pressure air) require welding to ASME B31.3 (Process Piping) standards.
  • Tank and Vessel Fabrication: Storage tanks (water, fuel oil) require welding certification to API 650 (Welded Tanks for Oil Storage) or equivalent standards.

3.5. Human Resource Requirements (BOP)

The BOP requires a broad spectrum of skilled technical staff, many of whom possess experience transferable from other large infrastructure projects.

  • Civil Maintenance Staff: Personnel skilled in maintaining concrete structures, roads, drainage, and utility tunnels.
  • Water Chemistry Technicians: Responsible for operating the Water Treatment Plant (WTP), including monitoring water purity, chemical dosing, and managing waste streams.
  • Logistics and Warehouse Managers: Essential for managing the enormous inventory of spare parts and consumables required for a 60-year plant life.
  • Security and Fire Safety Personnel: On-site teams trained in plant-specific security protocols and emergency response, including the operation of the dedicated Fire Protection System.

Strategic partnerships, human capital investment, and progressive localization will integrate Kenya into the nuclear energy world, fostering high-tech industries and skilled labor.
